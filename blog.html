Asynchronous Processing & Callback functions

Wednesday, July 29 2015

This is my understanding of asynchronous processing.

Lets consider a boss. The boss has 20 tasks to do today. Some tasks are easy and quick, others are more involved and require errands, communicating with other people. Not all tasks are created equally. So he completes task one. Easy. Task two. Easy. But then he gets to task three. Task three is complicated and should be broken into 2 parts: part a, and part b.

Hmmm, this task is complicated. Brian usually does part A for me and then hands over the result so I can do part B. But Brian takes soooooooooooooo long to do part A. I'm never going to get to task 4 if I wait for him to finish.
What to do. What to do.
Well, I'll just give Brian the instructions for how to do part B so he can do it himself and I'll carry on to task 4. Perfect!
So the boss tells Brian to do task 3A, like normal, but also gives him the instructions for what to do with he is done. The boss carries on to task 4. He does 10 more tasks until task 15 when he has the same dilemma....
No problem, Casey usually handles tasks like this. I'll let her handle it, like normal. But this time I'll also pass her the instructions for what to do when she's done.
So he does, and carries on. He finishes all 20 tasks, and then a few moments later Brian walks in with the results for task 3, and after that Casey with results from task 15.
This is how I understand asynchronous processing. And the important part in this analogy is the passing of instructions . The passing of instructions is the callback function. The boss says to Brian-- in this analogy Brian is a method in another module-- hey Brian, can you go and calculate all that stuff for me like you would normally, but also I am going to give you the instructions for what to do after so I don't have to wait for you to finish your calculations. Thanks.

So if we look at the below code:
var http = require('http');

http.get(url, function (response) {
response.setEncoding('utf8')
response.on('data', function(data) { console.log(data); });
response.on('error', console.error)
})

This is saying:
"Hey you! http module! Can you make a get request with this url ( http.get(url, ..) ) and get a response for me? Oh also, I don't really have time to wait for you to get the response.... so here's the instructions for what to do with that response object-- the function you can ..call back.. when you get that response object:
( .. function (response) {
/*do some stuff with response*/
});.

If starting out it might be easier to stomach the code this way:

var foo = function (response) {
/*do some stuff with response*/
}
http.get(url, foo);

Of course there are some obvious questions. What happens when the tasks are dependent on eachother? Do we just shove everything in the callback? What about nested callbacks? How do we ensure that Brian & Casey complete those instructions? (I smell promises...)

Women Who Code NYC Meetup -- Lightning Talks

Tuesday, July 7 2015

Bienvenidos a Nueva York! I intend to keep most of my posts technical, but for my first one I want to write about the Women Who Code meetup I had the opportunity to attend yesterday.

I saw 10 lovely ladies speak. It was my first event like this with all women, and I loved it. Of course diversity of all kinds of is necessary for great collaboration, but there is something very relaxing about working with women, as a woman. One of the reasons I love programming so much is because of the lack of discrimination and BS that, comparitively, seems to accompany other professions. In programming I often feel the premise tends to be, "Can you make it work? Great." No one cares who you are, what you look like, where or even if you went to college, just prove you have the skills and enjoy making things work. Being surrounded by action-oriented women extends the attitude of minimizing BS. It inspires creation and acceptance --even encourgement-- of failure to dominate the culture of thinking. (Maurya Couvares, the founder of ScriptEd , even spoke about the importance of learning how to fail in the context of her company). Its just nice to be with your peeps.

Speakers discussed the technical-- js promises, ES6 features , overview of Swift, web accessibility -- and nontechnical alike.

One thing worth noting were 2 vibrant ladies who have recently started the company monarq-- a friendship app for women. Even more interestingly, they also started a women's hackathon which it think is fan-freaking-tastic. I definitely intend on going to in October.

Another women's oriented world-wide collaboritive event that was discussed was Womens Entrepreneurship Day .
